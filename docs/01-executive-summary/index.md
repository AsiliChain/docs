AsiliChain addresses two linked constraints in African coffee value chains: extraction by intermediaries and exclusion from formal credit.

AsiliChain is underwriting infrastructure for African smallholder coffee - the data layer that makes a class of borrowers legible to formal capital for the first time.

Africa produces roughly 12 percent of the world's coffee and is home to the crop's genetic origin in Ethiopia. Uganda, the continent's second-largest producer, generates over one billion US dollars in annual export revenues from a sector built on the labour of 3.5 million smallholder farmers who remain among the most financially excluded people on earth. They earn approximately twenty US cents per kilogram of green coffee while the same kilogram retails at over ten dollars in European markets. The difference is not market efficiency. It is structural extraction, sustained by the absence of a verifiable record that could make these farmers visible to formal capital.

Three interlocking failures sustain this invisibility. Paper-based, opaque supply chains make provenance impossible to prove, generating quality disputes, rejected shipments, and zero compliance with the EU Deforestation Regulation. The absence of verifiable records means farmers carry no collateral and therefore no access to formal credit, trapping them in dependence on predatory informal lenders. And without a direct stake in quality and traceability, the incentive structure from farm gate to export container is broken for every participant except the middlemen who aggregate in the gaps.

The EU Deforestation Regulation (EU Regulation 2023/1115), enforced from 2025 onward, has made this invisible infrastructure suddenly visible and commercially urgent. Every shipment of coffee entering the EU must now be accompanied by a Due Diligence Statement with GPS polygon coordinates for every farm in the supply chain. For African producing countries whose supply chains are built on paper records and informal aggregation, this is an existential market access risk.

AsiliChain resolves all three failures simultaneously through a hybrid multi-chain architecture that separates two genuinely different needs: maximum simplicity for farmers interacting via USSD and feature phones, and maximum institutional credibility for regulated cooperatives, MFIs, governments, and European buyers who need compliance-grade infrastructure. The same on-chain record that satisfies a European auditor unlocks a farmer's working capital loan in February, months before their October harvest.
