## The Problem AsiliChain Does Not Yet Name

The whitepaper has, until this point, described AsiliChain primarily from the farmer's perspective and the lender's perspective. There is a third structural failure in the coffee value chain that the protocol addresses but has not made explicit: the information asymmetry between producers and buyers that middlemen exploit to capture the majority of value in the chain.

A cooperative in Mbale knows what their coffee costs to produce. A roaster in Amsterdam knows what they are willing to pay. Between them sits a chain of exporters, brokers, and commodity traders whose primary economic function is controlling the information that neither side has access to: forward prices, buyer demand, batch availability, and quality benchmarks. That information asymmetry is not a market efficiency. It is the structural basis of middleman extraction, and it persists because neither the cooperative nor the buyer has had a shared, trusted ledger on which to transact directly.

AsiliChain builds that ledger. The committed purchase order model is how it uses it to eliminate the information gap without requiring either party to give up the operational roles - quality assurance, logistics, cooperative management - that genuinely add value.
